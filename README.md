# **A lot of pollock is fished in Alaska. Almost none stays there.**
*looking into how much pollock leaves Alaska... and its a lot*

## Alaska's resources are obviously bountiful, and as a result extensively exploited. 

### I was curious *who* was moving fish out of Alaska and *where* it was going. And, a lot of the vessels shipping out pollock use predatory FOCs, or "flags of convenience," a growing issue in global maritime trade. 

### So, I began by trying to understand how much of *each stock* was leaving Alaska during their respective fishing seasons. I then decided to focus on pollock since so much of it gets shipped... well, everywhere. Millions of kilograms head, particularly, to South Korea, Japan and China, where the fish is used to make imitation crab. Global Russian pollock reliance is rapidly increasing, making an exploration into where Alaskan pollock is sold even more timely.

### Using ImportGenius data I was able to get a better picture of the route pollock takes once it leaves Alaska. The data is **surely incomplete**, since much doesn't even get registered to a port before leaving (and ImportGenius uses port registration to track shipments). I enjoyed using this very cool database to draw some interesting conclusions and get a better idea of how expansive this particular trade is.


### The social and economic problems caused by resource exploitation go hand in hand with environmental disregard. The ecosystem in the Bering Sea is under great duress — very likely only becoming more and more dire — as a result of bottom trawling. So, further work on this topic might include looking more into dark money in Alaska, bottom trawling stats in relevant waters and more on the environmental impacts of such aggressive fishing.
